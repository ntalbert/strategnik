Case Study 02

Building a Propensity Scoring System That Actually Works

How Data Governance Made Predictive Models 30% More Accurate

The Situation

An enterprise network security company had a sophisticated go-to-market motion—at least on paper. Salesforce for CRM. Marketo for automation. DemandBase for ABM and intent data. LinkedIn Ads for paid targeting. Four platforms, significant annual spend, and dashboards that could slice data any way you wanted.

The company's value proposition was clear: when businesses update their infrastructure—new POS systems, supply chain software, edge computing deployments—their network security architecture often can't keep up. These inflection points create vulnerability windows. The sales team knew exactly when to have the conversation: right after a major infrastructure change, when the prospect realizes their security posture needs to be re-evaluated.

The problem? The propensity model couldn't identify these moments. Sales didn't trust growth's "hot leads." Growth pointed to high intent scores. Sales pointed to conversations that went nowhere.

Both were right. The model was mathematically sound. It was also practically useless—because it was missing the technographic signals that actually predicted buying behavior.

The initial engagement was to fix demand gen, but within the first two weeks, it became clear that the problem wasn't campaigns or content or targeting. The problem was data—specifically, the absence of infrastructure change signals that would indicate an opportune time to approach.

The Insight

Propensity models are only as good as the data feeding them. Garbage in, garbage out—everyone knows this. But the failure mode here was two-fold: inconsistent data and missing data.

Problem 1: Data Inconsistency

The four systems had been implemented at different times by different teams with different naming conventions:

  • Salesforce had "IBM Corporation" as an account. Marketo had "IBM" and "IBM Corp" as separate records. DemandBase had "International Business Machines."
  • Industry classifications didn't match. Salesforce used a custom picklist. DemandBase used SIC codes.
  • The same contact might exist in three systems with three different titles.
  • Intent signals from DemandBase weren't consistently flowing to Salesforce.

Problem 2: Missing Technographic Signals

The model had no visibility into the infrastructure changes that actually triggered buying behavior. Sales knew from experience that certain events signaled an opportune moment to engage:

  • New POS terminal providers – retail and hospitality accounts upgrading payment infrastructure
  • New supply chain software – logistics and manufacturing accounts connecting new systems
  • New edge computing deployments – any account pushing compute to distributed locations
  • Fiber availability – whether the building housing the account's servers had fiber connectivity (prerequisite for their solution)

These signals weren't in DemandBase. They weren't in Salesforce. They existed in disparate technographic databases and public infrastructure records, but nobody had connected them to the propensity model.

The insight: The prediction problem was a data governance problem AND a signal coverage problem.

The System

Layer 1: Data Audit and Mapping

First, every field across all four systems was mapped. The output was brutal:

  • 47 fields that should have been synchronized
  • 23 fields with naming inconsistencies
  • 12 fields with conflicting data types
  • 3 systems with no automated sync

Layer 2: Standardization Rules

Company Identification

  • Primary key: Domain name (most reliable unique identifier)
  • Secondary matching: Legal entity name with fuzzy matching
  • Standardization: All company names normalized to D&B format

Industry Taxonomy

  • Standard: Adopted DemandBase industry categories as source of truth
  • Mapping: Built translation tables from SIC codes and Salesforce picklists

Layer 3: Technographic Signal Collection

A new data pipeline was built to capture infrastructure change signals:

Technology Change Detection:

  • POS Systems: Monitoring for new payment processor integrations (Square, Clover, Toast, etc.)
  • Supply Chain: Tracking new ERP and logistics platform deployments (SAP, Oracle, Manhattan Associates)
  • Edge Computing: Identifying accounts deploying IoT gateways, CDN infrastructure, or distributed compute
  • Cloud Migration: AWS, Azure, GCP adoption signals indicating infrastructure modernization

Infrastructure Readiness:

  • Fiber Availability: Cross-referenced account addresses against fiber infrastructure databases to confirm the building could support the solution
  • Data Center Proximity: Geographic analysis of account locations relative to network points of presence

These signals were enriched using technographic data providers and mapped to account records using domain-based matching. An open-source ETL pipeline (Airbyte) orchestrated the data flows between sources and the warehouse.

Layer 5: The Propensity Model

With clean, synchronized data and new technographic signals, the scoring model was rebuilt:

Input Signals (weighted by predictive power):

  • Technographic Change: Recent POS, supply chain, or edge computing deployments (highest weight)
  • Infrastructure Readiness: Fiber availability at primary location, data center proximity
  • Firmographic: Industry alignment, employee count, existing tech stack, geography
  • Behavioral: Website visits by page value, content downloads, email engagement
  • Intent: DemandBase surge scores, keyword research, competitor signals
  • Historical: Similarity to closed-won accounts

The model heavily weighted technographic change signals because historical analysis showed that accounts with recent infrastructure changes were 3.2x more likely to convert than accounts with high intent scores alone.

Layer 6: Vertical Segmentation

Rather than one model to rule them all, the system was calibrated for high-value verticals with specific technographic triggers:

Retail/Hospitality: Heavy weight on POS terminal changes, seasonal adjustment for post-holiday infrastructure upgrades

Logistics/Manufacturing: Supply chain software deployments, warehouse management system changes, IoT sensor rollouts

Banking/Financial Services: Compliance-driven infrastructure updates, ATM network modernization, branch connectivity changes

Healthcare: EHR migrations, telehealth platform deployments, medical device connectivity

The Takeaway

Most propensity models fail because they're missing the signals that actually predict buying behavior. Intent data tells you someone is researching. Technographic change data tells you they're ready to act.

1. Find your trigger signals. For this network security company, infrastructure changes (new POS, supply chain software, edge deployments) were 3x more predictive than intent scores. Every product has trigger events—find yours.
2. Cross-reference with readiness. A trigger signal without infrastructure readiness (in this case, fiber availability) is a false positive. The best leads had both: a recent change AND the ability to implement the solution.
3. Audit before you build. A month was lost before realizing the model wasn't the problem. Starting with a data audit—and a clear articulation of what signals matter—would have saved significant time.
4. Domain is your friend. Email domain as the primary key for matching solved most of the account matching challenges when combining data from multiple sources.

*The 2019 marketing org chart was organized by channel. The 2026 org chart is organized by outcome. The difference isn't cosmetic — it reflects a fundamental shift in what marketing teams actually need humans for.*

I posted an org chart comparison on LinkedIn last week that got more engagement than anything I've published this year. Not because the idea was novel — most senior marketing leaders already feel this shift — but because nobody had drawn it yet. Seeing the old structure next to the new one made the change concrete in a way that abstract conversation about "AI transformation" never does.

The old chart had six boxes reporting to the CMO: Paid Media, SEO, Content, CRM, Sales, Brand. Wide and flat. One person per channel. Optimized for execution.

The new chart has three outcome-oriented pods — Acquisition, Engagement & Monetization, and Brand — sitting on top of three cross-cutting architect layers: Orchestration, Creative and Voice System, and Data.

Deep and stacked. Optimized for decisioning.

The reaction split cleanly into two camps. People running modern teams said "this is exactly what we're building." People still hiring for the old model said "you can't replace a senior SEO strategist with AI." Both are right, and both are missing the point.

## What Actually Collapsed

Let's be specific about what happened, because "AI replaces jobs" is lazy and wrong. AI didn't replace people. It collapsed the skill premium on certain specialties.

**Product marketing** used to be the translation layer between product and market. Positioning documents, competitive battlecards, sales enablement decks, launch plans — all of it required a person who could hold the product's technical reality in one hand and the buyer's decision framework in the other. That translation work was genuinely hard and genuinely scarce.

It's not scarce anymore. An AI agent with access to your product docs, win/loss data, competitive intelligence, and CRM can produce a first draft of positioning, a battlecard, or a launch brief that would have taken a PMM a week. Not a perfect draft. A first draft that's 80% there — which means the remaining 20% is judgment, not production. You don't need a dedicated PMM to apply judgment. You need a strategist who can evaluate and sharpen output.

**Design** followed the same arc. The bottleneck in marketing design was never taste — it was production. Resizing assets, building landing page variations, creating social graphics, adapting templates for different channels. AI handles all of that now. The design skill that still matters is brand architecture: defining the system that governs how everything looks and feels. That's a creative director function, not a production designer function. One person plus AI replaces a team of three or four.

**SEO** is the most dramatic collapse. Traditional SEO — keyword research, on-page optimization, technical audits, link building strategies — was a specialty because the rules were complex and always changing. Google's algorithm was a black box that rewarded people who studied it obsessively. Now AI tools run technical audits in seconds, generate schema markup, optimize content structure, and monitor rankings automatically. The strategic question — what should we be known for and how do we earn authority — is a brand and content strategy question, not an SEO question. It always was, frankly. The tactical complexity just obscured that.

## The Shift from Channel to Outcome

The 2019 org chart was built on a simple assumption: each channel requires deep specialist knowledge, so each channel gets a person. Paid media is different from SEO is different from content is different from CRM. True in 2019. Decreasingly true in 2026.

When AI absorbs the execution complexity of a channel, the channel stops being a specialty and starts being a distribution mechanism. You don't need an SEO person — you need someone who owns acquisition and uses SEO as one of several levers. You don't need a CRM person — you need someone who owns the existing customer relationship and uses lifecycle automation as one of several tools.

This is the shift from organized by channel to organized by outcome:

**Acquisition** owns the top of funnel. Paid, GEO/AEO, landing pages, conversion. This person thinks in terms of pipeline creation and cost of acquisition, not channel-specific metrics. They deploy AI agents to manage the channels and focus their own time on strategy, creative differentiation, and conversion architecture.

**Engagement & Monetization** owns the existing customer. Retention, activation, expansion, lifecycle. This is the role that used to be split across CS, lifecycle marketing, and product marketing — all of which were measuring different slices of the same thing: how much value does this customer extract and how do we compound it?

**Brand** owns brand in market. Creative direction, production, voice. Not brand in the old "awareness campaign" sense — brand as the system that makes everything the company produces feel like it comes from the same mind.

## The Architect Layers Are the Real Story

The three pods are obvious. The three architect layers underneath them are where the actual leverage lives.

**Orchestration** is the person (or small team) that owns workflows, agents, and automation across the entire org. This is the function that didn't exist in 2019 because there was nothing to orchestrate — each channel ran its own playbook. Now the playbooks are interconnected, the agents are cross-functional, and someone needs to design and maintain the system that connects acquisition signals to engagement actions to brand consistency. I've written about this role as the [Chief Marketing Orchestrator](/thinking/chief-marketing-orchestrator/) — it's the most important new function in marketing.

**Creative and Voice System** is the architect layer that owns brand guidelines, messaging framework, voice and tone, and ICP definition. This isn't a person who makes ads. This is the person who builds the system that ensures every piece of output — whether generated by a human or an AI agent — sounds like the same company. As AI generates more of the first drafts, this function becomes more critical, not less. Without it, you get a company that publishes 10x the content at 0.1x the coherence.

**Data** owns infrastructure, decisioning, and signal. Not analytics in the old "here's a dashboard" sense — data as the nervous system that tells the pods what's working, what's changing, and what to do next. This person builds the feedback loops that make the outcome pods self-correcting rather than reactive.

## What This Means for Headcount

Here's the part that makes people uncomfortable.

A 2019 marketing team at a Series B company might have had 8-12 people: PMM, SEO specialist, content writer, designer, demand gen manager, lifecycle/CRM person, brand marketer, marketing ops, maybe a couple of junior people filling gaps.

The 2026 version of that team — producing more output, with better targeting, across more channels — might be 4-6 people plus an agent fleet. Three pod leads (Acquisition, Engagement, Brand), an orchestration architect, and one or two specialists in the architect layers depending on where the company's complexity lives.

That's not a cost-cutting argument. It's a leverage argument. The same budget that used to fund 10 people doing execution now funds 5 people doing strategy and judgment, plus AI systems handling the production and optimization work those 10 people used to do manually.

The people who thrive in this structure are the ones who were always frustrated by the production bottleneck — the strategists who spent 80% of their time on execution they considered beneath their capability. They finally get to operate at the level they were hired for.

The people who struggle are the ones whose value was the execution itself. The SEO specialist who knew every technical audit checklist. The designer who was fast at asset production. The PMM who was really good at turning product specs into slide decks. Those skills are now table stakes for any AI tool.

## The CMO Changes Too

The 2019 CMO was wide and flat — managing six or more direct reports, each running an independent channel. The job was coordination: making sure the channels didn't contradict each other and allocating budget across them.

The 2026 CMO is deep and stacked — managing three outcome pods and ensuring the architect layers serve all of them. The job is decisioning: determining where to invest, what signals to act on, and how to compound outcomes across the full customer lifecycle.

This is a harder job. It requires someone who can think in systems, not campaigns. Someone who understands data infrastructure well enough to design feedback loops. Someone who can evaluate AI-generated output against brand standards and market reality. Someone who treats the marketing function as an engine, not a department.

I've argued before that [the CMO is being reforged](/thinking/chief-marketing-orchestrator/) into something bigger. The org chart is the physical manifestation of that reforging.

## So What

If you're building a marketing team right now — or restructuring one — stop hiring for channels. Hire for outcomes and architect layers.

If you have an SEO specialist, a PMM, and a dedicated designer, ask yourself: what would these three people do if AI handled 80% of their production work? If the answer is "not much," you have the wrong people. If the answer is "they'd finally have time to think strategically," you might have the right people in the wrong structure.

The org chart isn't a theoretical exercise. It's the single most concrete expression of what a company believes marketing is for. If your chart still looks like 2019, your team is optimized for a world that no longer exists.
